# Review Questions — Section 214: Quality of Life Matters

**Instructions:** Answer each question, then expand the details to check your answer.

## Question 1
Under the Chronic Abuser Alarm Procedures (P.G. 214-08), what triggers a location being classified as a chronic abuser, and what is the minimum delay before police response can be reinstated after termination?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

A location is classified as a chronic abuser when **3 or more alarms** in a **3-month period** are unnecessary or unfounded. After a TERMINATION NOTICE (PD310-121) is served, the minimum delay before reinstatement takes effect is **48 hours**. The CO may also require an alarm system or premises security survey before reinstatement. (P.G. 214-08)

</details>

## Question 2
A vehicle alarm has been sounding continuously. At what point can MOS take action, and what is the time of violation recorded on the OATH summons?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

MOS can take action after the vehicle alarm has been audible for **3 minutes**. The time of violation recorded on the OATH summons is the **end** of the 3-minute observation period. The mail-in fine is $280 and the maximum penalty is $2,100. If the alarm resets and sounds again, it is considered **one continuous action** — only one summons is issued. (P.G. 214-24)

</details>

## Question 3
What is the critical distinction between MOS authority to deactivate vehicle alarms versus building alarms?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

MOS **may** attempt to deactivate **vehicle alarms** (within the first 10 minutes, without intentionally damaging the vehicle). However, MOS are **NOT permitted** to deactivate **building alarms**. For building alarms that continue for **15 or more minutes**, MOS may issue a summons. ESU should be requested if MOS have difficulty accessing the engine or passenger compartment of a vehicle. (P.G. 214-25)

</details>

## Question 4
A tenant claims they were unlawfully evicted. They have no lease but have lived in the unit for five weeks. The person who changed the locks is present but refuses to let the tenant back in. What actions should the sergeant direct?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The law applies because the tenant occupied the unit for **30 or more consecutive days** (5 weeks exceeds 30 days), even without a lease. Since the violator is present but **refuses to permit reentry**, the sergeant should direct an **arrest**. The subject must be brought to criminal court for **prompt arraignment — NO DAT** is permitted. Unlawful eviction is a **Class A misdemeanor** but is **NOT fingerprintable**. The sergeant should also confirm there is no valid Warrant of Eviction or Government Order to Vacate. (P.G. 214-12)

</details>

## Question 5
What are the summons return dates for unlawful eviction cases by borough, and what is the minimum lead time?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

All return dates must be at least **21 days** out:
- **Manhattan/Bronx:** Tuesday
- **Brooklyn/Queens:** Thursday
- **Staten Island:** First Wednesday of the month

(P.G. 214-12)

</details>

## Question 6
A dog has bitten a pedestrian but has since fled the scene. The animal is no longer present. What documentation and notifications are required?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The **DANGEROUS ANIMAL/BITE REPORT (PD311-152)** must be prepared in **every instance**, even if the animal is gone. The **desk officer** must notify the **Department of Health and Mental Hygiene by phone** because the animal bit a human. If the offender (owner) is identified, process for **DAT** if eligible. The animal is never placed in the trunk of a Department vehicle. (P.G. 214-33)

</details>

## Question 7
Under P.G. 214-33, when is a member of the service authorized to use a firearm against a dangerous animal?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

A firearm may be used ONLY to protect from **imminent physical injury** when there is **no opportunity to retreat**. General use of force against animals is limited to situations necessary to prevent harm or injury. (P.G. 214-33)

</details>

## Question 8
What defines a "Chronic 311 Condition," and what triggers an immediate response? Describe the different supervisory review frequencies.

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

- **Chronic 311 Condition:** **5 or more** requests in a **28-day period** for the same condition at the same location
- **Immediate response trigger:** **5 or more** calls within an **8-hour period**

Supervisory review frequencies:
- **Hourly:** Desk officer manually refreshes the 311 system at least every hour
- **Daily:** Second Platoon Desk Officer reviews at **0800** for the previous 24 hours; CO directs daily callbacks (minimum **2 attempts** per caller)
- **Weekly:** Special Ops Lt updates the 28-day chronic conditions list
- **Monthly:** Special Ops Lt conducts an audit of 311 callers

Command Log entries are required at the **beginning and end** of each tour with the number of pending assignments. (P.G. 214-35)

</details>

## Question 9
A C.U.R.E. operation is being planned against a nightclub. The nightclub owner's only issue is refusing to attend community nightlife meetings. Is this sufficient grounds for the operation?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**No.** Refusal to attend nightlife meetings is specifically listed as **NOT sufficient grounds** for a C.U.R.E. operation. Other insufficient grounds include: property crimes beyond the establishment's control, and crimes where security acted properly. Valid grounds include complaint reports, summonses, arrests, 911/311 calls, general disorder, aided cases from intoxication/overdose, and a documented pattern of problems. (P.G. 214-39)

</details>

## Question 10
What is the required lead time for a C.U.R.E. NOTICE, and what are the three possible outcomes after the notice period?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The **C.U.R.E. NOTICE (PD635-121)** must be served at least **30 days** prior to the operation. Prior notice is NOT required when it would compromise an active investigation or pose a health/safety risk.

Within **30 days** of the notice, the CO determines one of three outcomes:
1. **Conduct the operation**
2. **Additional monitoring**
3. **No further action**

The **Chief of Patrol** must approve or disapprove through written endorsement. The operation is coordinated by the **Legal Bureau's Civil Enforcement Unit** and overseen by the **Mayor's Office of Nightlife**. (P.G. 214-39)

</details>

## Question 11
During an eviction executed by a city marshal, the marshal asks MOS to help break down a door. Property is being placed on the sidewalk. What should the sergeant direct?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The sergeant should direct that:
1. MOS will **NOT assist** in breaking the door or damaging property — the police role is to **preserve peace and protect life/property ONLY**
2. The marshal/sheriff or landlord is **NOT permitted** to place property on the sidewalk — a **vehicle must be on scene** and property loaded **immediately**
3. Execution of the warrant is the **sole responsibility** of the city marshal/sheriff
4. The marshal must force entry in the **least disruptive way**

The sergeant should ensure the Activity Log records: marshal's name/shield, time of entry, location, tenant name, and who is responsible for property. (P.G. 214-13)

</details>

## Question 12
MOS discover a derelict vehicle with New York plates. It is not needed as evidence. What is the step-by-step process, and what are the key timelines?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

1. Remove the registration plates
2. Prepare the **REGISTRATION PLATE REMOVAL NOTICE (PD571-120)** (for NY plates)
3. Notify the **Stolen Property Inquiry Unit** (required in ALL cases)
4. Notify the owner to pick up plates within **5 days**
5. If unclaimed after 5 days: forward plates and typed letterhead to **DMV**
6. Desk officer assigns a check **5 days later** to verify if the vehicle is still present
7. If still there: notify the **Department of Sanitation** for removal

The vehicle is NOT taken into police custody because it is not needed as evidence. The Department of Sanitation removes all derelict vehicles not required as evidence. (P.G. 214-29)

</details>

## Question 13
MOS encounter potentially obscene material at a storefront. What is the correct chain of action, and what legal distinction must the supervisor understand?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

MOS must **NOT** take enforcement action. They must **immediately notify the patrol supervisor**. The patrol supervisor must:
1. Respond to the scene
2. Determine if the material meets legal criteria
3. Make an Activity Log entry
4. **Consult the Legal Bureau**

**No enforcement** is permitted without specific direction from the Legal Bureau.

The critical legal distinction:
- **PL Article 235 (Obscenity):** Requires a **judicial determination** that the item is obscene — no summary enforcement
- **PL 245.11 (Public Display of Offensive Sexual Material):** A **Class A misdemeanor** — summary enforcement is allowed under appropriate circumstances

(P.G. 214-11)

</details>

## Question 14
What are the eligibility requirements for the Mediation Referral Program, and what types of disputes are NOT eligible?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Eligibility requirements:**
- No threat of violence
- No criminal allegation
- No Order of Protection between the parties
- Parties are not family or intimate partners
- Likely an ongoing dispute

**NOT eligible:**
- Criminal conduct
- Unlawful evictions (handled under P.G. 214-12)
- Intimate partners or family members
- Child abuse or neglect
- Parties with an Order of Protection between them

The program uses the **REFERRAL TO MEDIATION (PD613-181)** with a serial number in **YYYY-XXX-NNNN** format. It must NOT be used in lieu of enforcement action — only when there is no basis to take enforcement action. The **Special Ops Lt** conducts a weekly inspection of the precinct mediation file. (P.G. 214-40)

</details>

## Question 15
A DOB inspector arrives at the precinct with an Access Warrant to inspect a commercial building. What must the desk officer verify, and what are the limits on MOS authority during the inspection?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The desk officer must verify:
1. The warrant is **signed by a Supreme Court judge**
2. The warrant is **not expired**

Limits on MOS authority:
- MOS role is to **preserve peace and prevent crime ONLY**
- MOS will **NOT** assist in breaking a door or damaging property
- If access is not granted by the occupant, **DOB reschedules** — this is not a police responsibility

The same principle applies to other civil enforcement (P.G. 214-13, 214-18): police preserve peace but do not participate in the execution of civil process. (P.G. 214-37)

</details>

## Question 16
A landlord has cut off hot water to a tenant who has lived in the apartment for two months without a lease. The tenant calls police claiming unlawful eviction. Is this an unlawful eviction?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**No.** While the tenant has legitimate occupancy (30+ consecutive days), cutting off **hot water** alone is NOT an unlawful eviction. The exam has tested this directly. Essential services that qualify as unlawful eviction when denied include **heat**, **water**, and **electricity** — but **hot water** by itself does not meet the threshold. The acts must also be done for the **purpose** of eviction (per LBB Vol 49 #1). (P.G. 214-12)

</details>

## Question 17
A city marshal arrives to execute a Warrant of Eviction at 1830 hours on a Wednesday. The tenant has a Stay of Eviction. The eviction was already completed earlier that day. What should the sergeant consider?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Two key issues:

1. **Time:** Warrants of Eviction may only be executed **Monday-Friday 0800-1700**. At 1830 hours, the execution window has passed. (Do NOT confuse with Search Warrant hours of 0600-2100.)

2. **Stay of Eviction:** Once the eviction is **complete**, a Stay of Eviction is **unenforceable**. Attempts to pay rent or produce a stay does NOT allow re-entry after the eviction is complete.

Additionally, per LBB Vol 49 #1, police **cannot break in** to let the tenant re-enter **UNLESS** there is medicine inside that is vital to life. (P.G. 214-13)

</details>

## Question 18
During a 311 assignment review, you notice a condition has been deferred. Where should the deferral be indicated — in the Telephone Dispatch Log, the Telephone Record, or the 311 system?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

Deferrals should be indicated in the **311 system** or the **Telephone Record**, as appropriate. The deferred assignment gets an interim disposition of "Deferred" or "Open." Do **NOT** confuse the Telephone Record with the Telephone Dispatch Log — the exam specifically tests this distinction. Notifications are documented in the Telephone Record. (P.G. 214-35)

</details>

## Question 19
Under P.G. 214-33, ESU is called to capture a dangerous dog. How is the animal transported, and what specific unit should be contacted if a search warrant is planned at a location known to have a dangerous dog?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Transport:** **ESU bags the animal** and then an **RMP transports** the animal to an ACC facility. The kennel is placed in the **backseat** of the RMP (rear seat may be removed if necessary). An animal is **NEVER** placed in the trunk of a Department vehicle.

**Search warrant:** If there is advance information of a dog at a warrant location, confer with the **Canine Unit of ESU** — not just any ESU team. (P.G. 214-33)

</details>

## Question 20
Who directs the NCO Sergeant to make daily callbacks to identified 311 callers — the Special Ops Lt or the CO? How many attempts are required per caller?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The **CO personally directs** the NCO Sergeant to make daily callbacks to identified 311 callers. This breaks the normal chain of command — it sounds like a Special Ops Lt responsibility, but the PG specifically assigns this to the CO. A minimum of **2 attempts** per caller is required, and all contacts and attempts must be documented. The NCO Sgt then interviews callers to determine if assignments were resolved or need further action, and confers with the SOL. (P.G. 214-35)

</details>

## Question 21
A complainant in an unlawful eviction case refuses to sign the summons. Can the UMOS still proceed? What modifications are needed when a civilian is the complainant?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

**Yes.** If the complainant refuses to sign, the **UMOS can sign** "based on information and belief" — but only if the information is **complete**.

When a **civilian is the complainant**, the following modifications are required on the summons:
- Cross out "Complainant's" and "Complainant" and substitute "Officer's" and "Officer" on the front
- Cross out "I personally observed..." above the complainant's name
- On the rear of the original summons, enter the complainant's full name, rank/full signature, and "Date Affirmed" under the appropriate sections
- Complainant's name, address, and phone number go on the bottom

(P.G. 214-12)

</details>

## Question 22
When referring a 311 condition to another city agency, what is the correct procedure? Can the referral be made anonymously?

<details><summary>Answer</summary>

The referral **cannot** be made anonymously. The correct procedure is:

1. Call **311 directly**
2. Provide: member's **name**, **Dept email**, and **smartphone number** (or TS phone number)
3. Obtain a **new Service Request number**
4. Make the original request disposition **10-95** ("Refer to Other Agency")
5. Put the new service request number in **"Notes to Customer"** before finalizing the old request

(P.G. 214-35)

</details>
